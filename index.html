Part 1

Skills of a Project Manager and me

≈1,000 words · 1st person

LO1, LO2, LO4 · 1.1 due weeks 1–3, 1.2 started weeks 1–3 and finished weeks 10–12.

1.1 — Knowledge and skills needed (10%)

Build one table covering three categories, each with real references attached — not a list of buzzwords:

  • Soft skills — teamwork, leadership, communication, negotiation, conflict handling
  • Organisational skills — planning, time management, prioritisation, delegation
  • Technical skills — scheduling software, budgeting tools, and explicitly, use of AI

Where to find citable material fast (the readings the module points you to):

A-grade signal: two sources that partly disagree (e.g. Golden Thread's broad list vs. PMI/PwC's finding that people skills top senior leaders' rankings) and a sentence saying what that tension means for you, beats five sources that all just get name-dropped.

1.2 — Reflect on your own knowledge, skills and competencies (15%)

Weeks 1–3: critically analyse your current ability against every row in your 1.1 table. Complete a CPD-style table or planner with reflections on prior project experience — paid work, placements, society committees, sports teams, group coursework all count.

Weeks 10–12: close the loop — what did you actually learn, across soft/organisational/technical skills, and what did your Study Buddies Buddycheck feedback tell you that you didn't already know about yourself?

Weak version: a claim with no evidence — "my communication skills are great, I present confidently and write well." A marker can't verify or credit a bare assertion.
Stronger version: claim → evidence → explanation → honest gap. State the skill, ground it in a specific real example, explain what that experience actually built, and name one thing that's still developing.

Structure each skill entry the same way every time: claim evidence explanation honest gap or growth. Optional: a simple before/after bar chart or scorecard (1–5 per skill) — this doesn't count toward the word limit and gives markers an instant visual of your development.

Things to avoid, straight from the brief: exaggerating in either direction — overselling or being needlessly hard on yourself. Both read as un-reflective rather than honest.

2.1 — Proposal for managing events as projects (15%)

The brief gives you four bullet points to hit, in order. Treat each as its own short sub-argument with a reference, not a paragraph of opinion.

① Why should event hosting be run as a project? Don't argue this in the abstract — use Easter on the Farm as your evidence base, then map what went wrong onto recognised project characteristics:

Maylor & Turner (2022, p.8) characteristicWhere you saw it at Easter on the Farm
Temporary / fixed start-end4-day event, but nobody planned it as bounded work with an end
Emergence & uncertaintyFootfall (750→5,800) was never estimated in advance
IntegratingRestaurant, juice factory, marquee and stallholders all had to interlock — and didn't
Change / impacts peopleStaff resignation, near-miss in the car park, insurance gaps
Social constructionFamily disagreement over whose responsibility parking and stock-outs were

You can make the identical point using the PM triangle / PMI (2017, p.580) constraints instead or as well — scope crept weekly in the run-up, nobody owned a schedule baseline, and quality (queues, stock-outs) suffered because resources weren't ring-fenced.

② Propose PM processes, roles and governance for Aruthiana. Anchor this to what the board already asked for in the transcript — they want a single decision-maker, ring-fenced core-operation staff, and a gate before the event. Useful structure to propose:

  • A named project sponsor (Clara, per the brief — you report to her) and a defined PM authority level
  • Stage gates mirroring the sprint milestones already used in the Apple Day plan — sign-off points where the sponsor formally approves before the next sprint starts
  • A lightweight change control step — any new "nice-to-have" needs sponsor sign-off and a budget line, full stop (this directly prevents the William-adds-a-band problem from the transcript)
  • Ring-fenced resourcing — operational staff (kitchen, juice factory) stay on BAU; event staffing is separately costed

③ Explain the need for stakeholder management, and design an approach. This is the standard power/interest grid (commonly attributed to Mendelow, 1991) shown in your workshop slides:

Low interestHigh interest
High powerKeep satisfied — enough involvement to avoid surprisesKey player — manage actively, highest priority
Low powerMonitor only — don't overload with updatesKeep informed — sustain interest, leverage when useful

Design this as a reusable organisational approach (a stakeholder register template + a standing communication cadence per quadrant), not a one-off list — the brief asks for something Aruthiana can reuse on future events. Pull real candidate names from the Case Data Vault stakeholder roll-call below rather than inventing generic ones.

④ Examine the significance of risk management, and design an approach. Reference the standard four-stage cycle (plan → identify → analyse → risk response → implement) and the probability/impact grid from your slides. Worth naming explicitly: ISO 20121 is the international standard specifically for sustainable event management — genuinely relevant here given the brief's own sustainability requirement, and a strong, precise reference to use.

Tool skill, not theory

The MS Planner workshop

Everything below maps the case study's own task list onto Microsoft Planner's actual views, verified against the current (2026) planner.cloud.microsoft interface. Build this once — it feeds both halves of Part 3.

Check this before you type a single task in. Timeline (Gantt), dependencies, milestones and sprint buckets are all Premium features. If your plan was created as a Basic plan, the Timeline tab won't appear even with a premium licence attached to your account — the plan itself has to be created as Premium/Advanced from the start. When you create your plan, look for an explicit "Premium"/"Advanced" plan type option, or duplicate the shared "Events Expert App" plan type your seminar already used, which has Grid, Board, Charts and (with the right licence) Timeline.

Access & setup

  1. Go to planner.cloud.microsoft and sign in with your ARU account.
  2. New plan → name it Apple Day Big Weekend Preparation. Choose the Premium/Advanced plan type (see warning above).
  3. In Board view, create five buckets, named exactly as the case study's sprints so your Board reads as a direct translation of the brief:
Bucket (= sprint)
Sprint 1: Concept and Foundations
Sprint 2: Bookings, Vendors and Ordering Supplies
Sprint 3: Marketing and Operations
Sprint 4: Farm Preparation
Sprint 5: Brief Aruthiana Fruits Management on the Setup
On "summary tasks": the brief asks for sprints to show as buckets in Board view and as summary tasks in Timeline/Grid. Planner's Timeline doesn't generate true indented roll-up bars the way classic MS Project does — its structure is bucket-based, not a WBS. The direct equivalent is to switch on Group by Bucket in both Grid and Timeline views, so each sprint appears as a visual grouping header over its tasks. Say this explicitly in your 3.1 write-up — naming the limitation and how you worked around it is itself evidence of software fluency, which is exactly what LO4 wants.

Building the schedule

  1. Switch to Grid view. Add columns for Start date, Duration and Predecessor if not already visible.
  2. Enter every task from the map above, into its matching bucket. Set T1's start to 29 June 2026 — the fixed project start date given in the brief.
  3. Set Predecessor for each task using Finish-to-Start dependencies exactly as mapped — this is what makes Planner recalculate downstream dates automatically instead of you typing dates by hand.
  4. Tasks sharing a predecessor with no dependency between each other (e.g. T2 and T3, both only depending on T1) should run in parallel — don't chain them to each other or you'll invent a false dependency the case study never states.
  5. Add the four milestones as zero-duration tasks. Pin M4 to 7 October 2026 — this date is fixed by the board, not calculated from the chain.
  6. Switch to Timeline view. Toggle on Show critical path in the filters — this highlights the actual chain of tasks controlling your finish date, which is exactly what the crashing/critical-path theory from your workshops is asking you to apply in practice.
Sanity-check your own numbers. Using only the fixed durations above (ignoring the four undefined tasks for a moment), the longest chain runs: H&S (14d) → Menu (16d) → [M1] → Marketing campaign (21d) → Itinerary (14d) → [M3] → Farm site prep (21d) → Push marketing (2d). That's roughly 88 elapsed days from 29 June — landing around late September 2026, using simple 7-day weeks as an approximation (Planner will compute exact working-day dates once you build it, which will shift this a little). That leaves only about 1½–2 weeks before the fixed 7 October board review to fit your three undefined Sprint 5 tasks. If your Planner build shows a lot more slack than that, or a lot less, it's worth checking your dependencies before you screenshot anything.

Notice what this puts on your critical path: the Head Chef's menu development, the marketing campaign, the itinerary, and farm site prep — not the vendor bookings or ticketing, which have real slack. That's a genuinely useful management insight for your write-up: those are the tasks worth protecting if anything slips.

Assign each task to yourself as PM (since work-effort hours are costed to you specifically), and add named Aruthiana staff — Katarzyna Nowak, Rita Kumar, Hans Wagner — as collaborators on tasks where the case study says they're involved, so your People view actually reflects who's delivering what.

Common Planner pitfalls, from the seminar clinic: creating the plan as Basic (no Timeline tab appears); forgetting dependencies (bars sit disconnected and no critical path can be shown); mixing "weeks" and "days" inconsistently when typing durations; forgetting to pin M4 to the fixed 7 Oct date; and naming buckets generically ("To do / Doing / Done") instead of the five named sprints the brief asks for.
Part 4

Managing Implementation

≈600 words · 300 each · 3rd person

LO2, LO4 · Choose exactly 2 of the 13 scenarios below. For each: analyse repercussions, explain your response, and include a re-worked Timeline (Gantt) plus your management steps.

A repeatable structure for each of your 2 scenarios

  1. Diagnose — is this a constraint problem (scope/schedule/cost/quality/resources/risk, PMI 2017 p.580) or a project-characteristic problem (Maylor & Turner emergence, change, social construction etc.)? Name it precisely.
  2. Repercussions — trace the knock-on effect through your own Gantt chart from Part 3. Quantify where you can (which task moves, by how much, what becomes newly critical).
  3. Response — pick a real PM technique, not a generic "I would communicate with the team." If it's a threat, name the strategy: escalate, avoid, transfer, mitigate, or accept (PMI, 2021, p.123). If it's a schedule slip, this is a live crashing scenario — reference crash time/crash cost trade-offs.
  4. Re-worked Gantt — show the revised chart, and the 2–3 sentences explaining what changed and why.
